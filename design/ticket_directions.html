My Concert Atlas · Design Exploration

The ticket is the artifact.

Four directions, none of them iterations of the current cream rectangle. Each is a complete material world — its own paper, ink, typography, and motion — shown in all four states: full, upcoming, archived, and the compact journey overlay. Hover any ticket to pick it up.

Every ticket below is set with real events from the archive — from № 000001 (Live · New World Music Theatre, August 31 1995) to № 000642 (Counting Crows · Hollywood Bowl, August 7 2026). No seat, row, section, or price appears anywhere: the archive number derived from the event id is the only decorative field.

ILetterpress Archive
Philosophy

A keepsake printed on a proof press: heavy cotton paper, two inks (iron black and a worn rust), deep impression, a fleuron on the center rule. The year stands in a Didot column like a spine label. Influences: letterpress print shops, museum admissions, century-old theatre stock. This is the most archival direction — it treats every show like it already happened long ago, beautifully.

Motion

Paper laid on a table. The next ticket slides in from the right with a slight settling rotation while the previous slips underneath — a stack forming as the journey plays. Nothing bounces; paper has weight.

IIBoarding Pass
Philosophy

The journey mode already plays like a travel documentary — this ticket makes the document to match. Airline stock with a fine security tint, mono microtype labels, a big condensed city code, the year set like a flight number. The waveform strip is openly decorative (it will never scan). Influences: boarding passes, transit punch cards, mid-century airline liveries — no specific carrier.

Motion

Pulled from a document sleeve: passes slide laterally with a paper-drag ease, and the city code ticks over like a split-flap board as the journey crosses a border.

IIIGrandstand Stub
Philosophy

The shoebox ticket: aged manila stock with foxing, a faint sunburst behind the bill, navy and faded red inks, wood-type condensed capitals, a script flourish on at the, and a KEEP THIS COUPON stub. Influences: 1940s–60s grandstand and ballpark stubs, county-fair admissions, early rock handbills. The warmest and most nostalgic direction — every show becomes a night your grandfather might have kept.

Motion

Thumbing a stack in a shoebox: the current stub tips forward and away while the next rises from beneath it, slightly offset, like riffling through decades.

IVDie-Cut Nocturne
Philosophy

The deliberate risk: stop borrowing nostalgia and let the app print its own ticket. Night-black card with die-cut corners, a hairline of amber foil, and the year as a huge ghost numeral pressed into the stock. A cream paper band wraps the card and tears away when the show is archived. This is the only direction that shares a material world with the map itself — the journey overlay stops looking like a scan and starts looking like the app's signature object. Influences: premium gig laminates, letterpress-on-black studio prints, gallery admission cards.

Motion

Night transit: the current card dims and slides down and away while the next emerges out of the map's glow — the transition and the journey share one atmosphere.

Choosing

All four keep the exact information architecture you have today — year-dominant date, artist, venue, city, ALSO LISTED, appearance number, milestone, stamped status — so any one can replace the current renderer without touching the data layer.

Grandstand Stub is the most emotional artifact — closest to a shoebox of real stubs. Boarding Pass is the best structural fit for the travel-documentary journey mode. Letterpress Archive is the most timeless and the calmest. Die-Cut Nocturne is the only one that makes the ticket the app's own identity rather than a memory of someone else's — the boldest, and my recommendation for the journey overlay even if a paper direction wins the archive pages.

A split is also legitimate: one paper direction for archive and profile pages, Nocturne for the ticket that floats on the map.
